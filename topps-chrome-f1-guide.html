Topps Chrome F1 Guide

Everything a new collector needs to understand Topps Chrome Formula 1 — the flagship product that defines the F1 card hobby.

If F1 cards have a center of gravity, it's Topps Chrome Formula 1. This guide covers what it is, how the parallels work, the inserts collectors chase, and how to start a collection sensibly.

A short history

Topps launched its first licensed Topps Chrome Formula 1 set in 2020, bringing the familiar chrome-stock, refractor-driven formula — long beloved in other sports — to Formula 1 for the first time. The timing rode a wave of new fans, and the hobby has expanded quickly since, with annual flagship releases, e-commerce-exclusive editions, and ultra-premium products layered on top.

The Chrome formula

“Chrome” refers to the reflective card stock. Refractors are the signature chase: cards with a rainbow refractive finish that catches light. From there, Topps builds a ladder of parallels — color variations, patterns, and serial-numbered versions — that get rarer as you climb.

The parallel ladder

The exact lineup changes year to year, but the logic is consistent: a base card sits at the bottom, with progressively scarcer parallels above it. Many parallels are serial-numbered to a fixed print run (for example /99, /50, /25, /10, /5), and the scarcest tiers culminate in a one-of-one SuperFractor. As a rule, lower print run plus a more desirable color equals more value.

Insert sets

Beyond base and parallels, Topps Chrome F1 includes themed insert sets with their own designs and followings — cards like the Futuro and Neon Nations inserts among others. Striking, well-loved inserts often punch above plain base parallels at the same print run, and they each have their own refractor and numbered parallels too.

Sapphire Edition

Topps Chrome Sapphire Formula 1 is a sought-after, e-commerce-exclusive variant built on a distinctive glassy blue Sapphire stock. Its parallels and gem-graded copies are popular with collectors who want a different look and feel from the flagship.

Autographs

Autograph cards — especially on-card signatures and low-numbered parallels — sit near the top of the market. Pull rates are low, demand is high, and a signed refractor or numbered auto can be worth many multiples of its unsigned equivalent.

Beyond Chrome: the premium tier

For the high end, Topps also produces ultra-premium F1 products — most notably Topps Dynasty, known for autographed patch and relic cards numbered to tiny print runs, including one-of-ones. These are where many record-level F1 sales happen.

How to start collecting

  • Pick a lane. A favorite driver, a single set/year, or a specific parallel tier is far more satisfying (and affordable) than trying to buy everything.
  • Buy the card, not the hype. Check sold comps before paying. The Price Guide walks through how.
  • Mind condition. Chrome shows edge and surface flaws easily; inspect closely and consider grading for keepers.
  • Use trusted channels. Established marketplaces and auction houses, with sold-price verification, protect you from overpaying.
